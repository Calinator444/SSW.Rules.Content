### Introduction  
When you send an email or message, you might think you've covered everything but you often haven't. Then the recipient comes back with questions you *should* have anticipated. That wastes time, introduces confusion, and sends you both into extra loops. In his book The 4‑Hour Workweek, Tim Ferriss emphasises eliminating unnecessary back-and-forth (e.g., by batching email checks).

### Why you should intentionally include follow-up questions  
- **Reduces rework:** When you anticipate the next logical questions, you reduce the number of rounds of clarification.  
- **Demonstrates thought leadership:** It shows you've considered the wider implications; you aren’t just reacting.

### When to anticipate follow up questions
Anticipating follow ups isn't necessarily a panacea for unblocking you when you. 

- **Providing Context:** Sometimes your audience want to undestand the why before doing a task and not just the what.
- **Asynchrounous Communications:** Asynchrounous forms of communication such as emails and teams calls are the primary situation where failing to anticipate follow up questions can come back to bite you. If a byte of extra context is provided in a reply to your original email that changes the scope of the work you could have lost hours of your own time going down a rabbit hole you never should have explored.
